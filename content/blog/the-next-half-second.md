title = "The Next Half-Second"
date = 2026-05-19
+++

Jetlagged and loaded down with bags, you climb the dark porch steps to the rental. You cannot really see your feet. Each one swings up to the height of the next tread and lands flat without your looking, because somewhere beneath conscious thought your brain has already simulated where the step will be from the rhythm of the last few, half a second before your foot arrives.

When one step is taller than the last, or shorter than it looked, your foot meets air or hits early and you feel a little jolt of surprise. The model corrects, and the next half-second gets simulated a bit more accurately. One leading account of cognition calls this *predictive processing*: the brain is constantly generating the percepts it expects, checking them against what the senses actually report, and adjusting both its predictions and its motor commands on the fly. That loop has been running underneath every action you've ever taken since the day you were born.[^predictive-processing]

Watch a video of someone pouring water into a glass. You know before it reaches the rim whether it will overflow, and you can feel the weight of the pitcher in a hand you are not holding. You never touched it: watching was enough to run the simulation forward. A great deal of what you can predict, you learned from the outside like this, by watching the world act on itself.

A child learns physics from roughly 20,000 waking hours of unstructured sensory experience before she starts school: objects falling, liquid spilling over the lip of a cup, the way light slides differently across a wet surface than a dry one. The physics are never explained; they are felt, over and over, until the model stops being surprised. By the time she picks up a toy she has never seen before, those hours of reaching for things and letting them fall have built a model of how objects behave when you grip and lift.

LLMs got good at coding and math because the loop there is cheap and closes by itself. You write the code and run it, the error comes straight back, and you fix it while you still remember what you tried. The physical world has no such free loop. You'd need a simulator that renders what happens next and checks it against what actually happened. Every task that leans on physical reasoning needs that loop to close: a robot closing its fingers on an egg without crushing it, a prosthetic hand bracing for a mug it hasn't lifted yet.

"World model" may have surpassed "AGI" as the term everyone uses and nobody agrees on. Narrow RL agents learned world models inside Atari games and simulated Go boards in the 2010s. LeCun proposed a latent world model as the path to autonomous intelligence. Roboticists use the term to mean a dynamics model of a specific arm in a specific workspace, and the marketing departments of half a dozen startups use it to mean whatever they shipped last quarter.[^world-model-history]

Strip away the branding and the same machinery keeps showing up. A world model learns a compressed representation of how things behave, and then it has to run that representation forward to render what happens next in observable space. The useful part is that you can condition the rollout on an action, so it answers "what happens if I do X." And each time the rendering misses, the surprise feeds back in, so the next one is a little less wrong.

Video generation models are the best foundation because they train on the world itself: endless hours of people cooking, building, driving, failing and recovering, in every setting and light someone thought to film.[^gen45] They render in observable space, pixels and forces and textures, the weight of a pitcher before you tilt it, the slide of a wheel on wet asphalt. That gives you what a latent objective throws away: dense error signals tied to what sensors actually measure.

Early results bear this out: a humanoid trained on video world models performed 22 new behaviors in unseen environments from a single pick-and-place demonstration.[^dreamgen] Runway's GWM-Robotics, built on a video generation foundation, correlated at 0.95 with real-world policy outcomes across eight manipulation policies and 16,000 human evaluations, outperforming traditional real-to-sim approaches that require 3D scene reconstruction.[^gwm-robotics] The visual diversity of the training data is what lets them generalize. The same model that learned how liquids pour also learned how fabric drapes, how light shifts across a room at different times of day, how a stack of plates responds when you pull one from the middle.

The visual cortex evolved under the narrow pressure of predator detection and spatial navigation. The simulation machinery that learned to predict "is that shadow a leopard or a rock" turned out to be general enough to support reading, art and face recognition, none of which existed as selection pressures. But evolution had an advantage we do not: every generation trained on a body that could touch, lift and break things, with billions of years to close the loop. An artificial simulator starting from video has the visual diversity but not the embodiment, and no billions of years to grow it.

Whether a simulator can stay purely digital depends on the task. If the physics show up in video, a digital loop may be enough: a game designer checking whether a jump arc feels right, a filmmaker previewing a shot nobody has filmed. For robots it may have to cross into the physical world, where the predicted force has to match what the motor actually delivers. Today's models are vision-only. They miss touch and force and proprioception, the signals that matter most when a hand closes on an egg or a foot lands on ice. You feel the traction go before you consciously register the slip, and a model that has never felt the gap between the torque you command and the torque you get will just make contact up.

You can't build a world model in a vacuum. A simulator trained only in the lab gets good at the benchmark the lab designed, and a benchmark only measures the part of reality someone already thought to ask about. What's worth simulating, and how precisely, depends on what someone is trying to *do* with it. An architect needs the light right in a building that doesn't exist yet. A prosthetics engineer needs a hand that matches the weight and slip of real objects. A game designer needs plausible physics at frame rate across an open world. You find the tradeoffs only when real users push the thing past what the research team imagined.

The door swings open and you step into your rental. The hallway is dark. Your hand sweeps the wall to the right of the frame, fingers spread, because that is where light switches have been in every house you have ever entered. You find it. The lights come on. You have been simulating this whole time, half a second ahead of yourself.
